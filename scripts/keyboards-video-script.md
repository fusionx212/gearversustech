### SECTION 1: WHAT YOU ACTUALLY PAY (1:15–2:00)

**[VISUAL: Price comparison table on screen. Highlight the value outliers — K2 HE in green, Corsair in green.]**

Let's talk money.

The Wooting, Razer, and SteelSeries all sit in that £170 to £220 range. At that price, you're getting adjustable actuation — meaning you can set exactly how far you press a key before it registers — and rapid trigger, which lets you re-press a key without it fully resetting. If you play Valorant, CS2, or Apex at a competitive level, these features matter. If you don't, they're specs you'll never notice.

The Keychron K2 HE is the interesting one. £100 to £130 gets you hall effect switches, rapid trigger, adjustable actuation, AND wireless. It's the only wireless hall effect keyboard in this roundup. The trade-off is a plastic case and software that's not quite as polished. But that price gap — £70 to £90 less than the Wooting — pays for a decent mouse.

And then there's the Corsair K70 CORE at £70 to £90. Standard mechanical switches — no rapid trigger, no adjustable actuation, just a solid, well-built keyboard that works. If you're not grinding ranked, this is probably the sensible choice.

Five-year cost for all of these is near zero. Keyboards don't have subscriptions. You might replace the keycaps after a couple of years — budget £15 to £40 for a decent PBT set. But the real question is whether you'll still want this keyboard in 18 months when the next switch technology arrives. Hall effect boards have the longest relevance window right now.

### SECTION 2: THE PROBLEMS NOBODY TELLS YOU (2:00–4:30)

**[VISUAL: Cut to each keyboard as it's discussed. Red text overlays for the "bad" points. Show Reddit comments/quotes on screen when referenced.]**

Alright. This is the section that matters. Every keyboard here has flaws, and I'm going to tell you what owners actually complain about — not the Amazon review highlights, the real stuff from Reddit, forums, and people who've lived with these for months.

**[VISUAL: Wooting 80HE close-up.]**

**The Wooting 80HE.** The rapid trigger and Rappy Snappy tech is genuinely excellent. If you play competitive shooters, this gives you a measurable advantage. The Lekker switches come factory-lubed and they're remarkably smooth. And the software — Wootility — runs in your browser. No install. No background process eating your CPU. That alone puts it ahead of Razer and SteelSeries.

But. There's no wireless option. At £180, you're permanently tethered via USB-C. The stock keycaps are ABS and they'll develop shine within three to six months of heavy use — budget another £30 for a PBT set. And rapid trigger can feel properly weird for typing. Search "Wooting 80HE typing experience" on Reddit and you'll find people who bought it for gaming and genuinely regret using it for work. If you type as much as you game, think carefully.

**[VISUAL: Razer Huntsman V3 Pro close-up.]**

**The Razer Huntsman V3 Pro.** Gen-3 optical switches rated for 100 million keystrokes — effectively immortal. Adjustable actuation from 0.1 to 4mm, same as the hall effect boards. Comes in full-size, TKL, and 60% Mini — more size options than anyone else here.

But. You must run Razer Synapse. It's bloated, it phones home, and it has a documented history of chewing CPU cycles on some builds. The optical switches sound thin and pingy compared to hall effect or mechanical — almost hollow on the upstroke. And the keycap stems are proprietary Razer optical, so you can't use standard Cherry MX keycaps without adapters. For £180 to £220, you're locking yourself into Razer's ecosystem. If you're already deep in it — fine. If you're not, think about whether you want another piece of always-running peripheral software.

**[VISUAL: Keychron K2 HE close-up.]**

**The Keychron K2 HE.** This is the value winner and it's not particularly close. Hall effect switches with rapid trigger at almost half the price of the Wooting. Wireless via Bluetooth 5.1 with a 4,000 milliamp battery. Hot-swap sockets — if you get bored of hall effect, you can drop in standard mechanical switches tomorrow. And the Gateron Nebula switches come pre-lubed and sound deeper than the Wooting's Lekkers — they genuinely sound better.

But. 75% layout is cramped if you're used to TKL or full-size. You'll be doing Fn combos for home and end keys. Bluetooth latency is noticeable in fast-paced games — you'll use it wired for gaming, which defeats half the point. Keychron's launcher software is less polished than Wootility with fewer game profiles. And the plastic case feels exactly like what it costs — it doesn't have the premium weight or rigidity of the aluminium boards here. You're paying for the switches, not the chassis.

**[VISUAL: Corsair K70 CORE close-up.]**

**The Corsair K70 CORE.** Genuine mechanical switches under £90 with a solid aluminium top plate. Dedicated media controls that work without software — volume roller, mute button, plug and play. iCUE is optional, not required. This is a keyboard, not a software subscription.

But. No rapid trigger, no adjustable actuation. These are standard linear switches in a hall effect world. The keycaps are ABS with laser-etched legends that will fade — these are £5 keycaps on a £70 keyboard. No hot-swap sockets — the switches are soldered. If one fails after warranty, you're replacing the whole board. And the included wrist rest is hard plastic. Comfortable for about 30 minutes, then you'll want something with actual padding.

**[VISUAL: SteelSeries Apex Pro TKL close-up.]**

**The SteelSeries Apex Pro TKL.** OmniPoint 2.0 hall effect switches with per-key actuation. Built-in OLED display that shows game stats, Discord notifications, or custom GIFs — and I'll be honest, it's actually useful mid-game. Dual actuation lets you set one action on half-press and another on full-press — walk on light press, sprint on full. It's clever. And the aircraft-grade aluminium frame feels premium in a way the Keychron doesn't.

But. The OmniPoint switches have a slightly gritty feel on slow presses compared to Wooting's Lekkers. SteelSeries GG software is required for per-key configuration — no web-based alternative, and it's another background process you didn't ask for. The OLED screen adds cost for a feature most people stop using after the first week. And the USB passthrough is USB 2.0 only — it can't power anything beyond a mouse, which makes it borderline useless.

### SECTION 3: HEAD-TO-HEAD (4:30–5:30)

**[VISUAL: Full comparison table on screen. Highlight winning cells as they're discussed.]**

Let's put them side by side. I'll move quickly — pause if you need to.

**[Walk through table highlights, not every cell.]**

Switch technology: Wooting and Keychron use hall effect, Razer uses optical, Corsair is standard mechanical, SteelSeries is also hall effect. Hall effect is the current gold standard for gaming.

Adjustable actuation: All of them except the Corsair have it. If you want to tune your key sensitivity, the Corsair is out.

Rapid trigger: Wooting, Razer, Keychron, and SteelSeries all have it. Corsair doesn't. If you play competitive FPS, this is the feature that matters.

Polling rate: Wooting and Razer hit 8,000Hz. The others are at 1,000Hz. Honest opinion — 8,000Hz is nice but you will not feel the difference above 1,000Hz unless you're in the top 1% of competitive players. Don't buy a keyboard for this spec alone.

Wireless: Only the Keychron K2 HE has it. If wireless matters, your choice is made.

Software situation: Wooting wins here decisively. Browser-based Wootility means no install, no background process, no phoning home. Razer requires Synapse. SteelSeries requires GG. Corsair and Keychron have optional software — use it if you want, ignore it if you don't.

Build: SteelSeries and Razer feel the most premium. The Wooting is solid but understated. The Keychron is plastic and it shows. The Corsair punches above its price.

### SECTION 4: WHO SHOULD BUY WHAT (5:30–6:30)

**[VISUAL: Split screen. Left side green "Buy if", right side red "Avoid if". Rotate through each keyboard.]**

**Buy the Wooting 80HE if** you play competitive FPS — Valorant, CS2, Apex — and you want every possible advantage. Rapid trigger + 8,000Hz + browser-based software is the cleanest competitive package on the market. Also buy it if you hate peripheral software running in the background — Wootility being web-based is a genuinely underrated feature.

**Avoid the Wooting if** you need wireless, or if you type as much as you game. Rapid trigger makes typing feel wrong.

**Buy the Keychron K2 HE if** you want hall effect technology without spending £180. At £100 to £130, it's 85% of the Wooting for 60% of the price. Also the only wireless hall effect option. If you want customisation later, the hot-swap sockets let you change switches without soldering.

**Avoid the Keychron if** you play competitive shooters wirelessly — the Bluetooth latency will frustrate you — or if you want the premium feel of an aluminium chassis.

**Buy the Razer Huntsman V3 Pro if** you're already in the Razer ecosystem and Synapse is running anyway. You get size options nobody else offers. The optical switches will outlast every other switch here on paper.

**Avoid the Razer if** you hate always-running software or want custom keycaps. The proprietary stems lock you in.

**Buy the Corsair K70 CORE if** you want a proper mechanical keyboard under £90 and you don't care about rapid trigger. It's the sensible choice. Dedicated media controls that work without software are a quality-of-life feature you'll appreciate every day.

**Avoid the Corsair if** you play competitive FPS. No rapid trigger means you're leaving performance on the table.

**Buy the SteelSeries Apex Pro TKL if** you want hall effect in a premium aluminium body and you'll actually use the OLED display. The dual actuation feature is genuinely clever for games that support it.

**Avoid the SteelSeries if** you think the OLED is gimmicky — you're paying for it whether you use it or not — or if you want the smoothest hall effect switches, because Wooting's Lekkers are better-lubed from factory.

### VERDICT (6:30–7:15)

**[VISUAL: Winner podium animation. Wooting 80HE at top, Keychron K2 HE below with "Best Value" badge, Corsair K70 CORE with "Budget Pick" badge.]**

The Wooting 80HE is the best gaming keyboard of 2026. Not because it has the most features — it doesn't. Not because it's the best-built — the SteelSeries and Razer are arguably more premium. But because it delivers the best competitive gaming experience with the least friction. No software. No bloat. Just rapid trigger, 8,000Hz polling, and hall effect switches that feel excellent out of the box.

The compromises are real — no wireless, mediocre keycaps, and rapid trigger that's borderline unusable for typing. But for pure gaming performance, nothing beats it.

If £180 to £200 is too much, buy the Keychron K2 HE. You get 85% of the experience — including hall effect and rapid trigger — plus wireless, for £100 to £130. It's the smartest value in gaming keyboards right now.

And if you're spending under £90, buy the Corsair K70 CORE. It's a proper mechanical board with solid build quality and zero software requirement. You give up the competitive features, but if you're not grinding ranked, you won't miss them. Save the extra £100 and put it toward a better monitor or GPU.
