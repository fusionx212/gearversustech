## THE PROBLEMS (1:20–4:30)

### WOOTING 80HE

**SUBTITLE:** WOOTING 80HE — WHAT BREAKS

**[SHOT: Wooting close-up. Then cut to Reddit screenshot with highlighted text.]**

**SUBTITLE (bullet points appear sequentially):**
- No wireless at £180
- ABS keycaps shine within 3 months
- Rapid trigger makes typing feel wrong
- Software is browser-only — no offline config

**VO:** The Wooting is the best gaming keyboard here. It's also annoying in ways you should know about. No wireless. The keycaps will develop shine within three to six months — budget another £30 for a decent PBT set. And rapid trigger, the feature that makes it so fast in games, makes typing feel unsettling. Search "Wooting typing experience" on Reddit — people who bought it for gaming and work regret it.

**[SHOT: Screen recording of Wootility browser software. Smooth. Clean.]**

**SUBTITLE:** Wootility runs in your browser. No install. No background process.

**VO:** One thing Wooting gets right that nobody else does — the config software runs in a browser tab. No install, no background process, no phoning home. Razer, SteelSeries — take notes.

### RAZER HUNTSMAN V3 PRO

**SUBTITLE:** RAZER HUNTSMAN V3 PRO — THE SOFTWARE PROBLEM

**[SHOT: Razer keyboard. Then screen recording of Razer Synapse. Task Manager open showing CPU usage.]**

**SUBTITLE (bullet points):**
- Requires Razer Synapse — always running
- Optical switches sound thin and pingy
- Proprietary keycap stems — no custom caps
- 100 million keystroke rating — immortal switch, annoying software

**VO:** The Huntsman V3 Pro is a good keyboard trapped inside terrible software. Synapse is required. It phones home. It has a documented history of eating CPU cycles. And the optical switches — rated for 100 million keystrokes, which is effectively immortal — sound thinner than the hall effect alternatives. Pingy on the upstroke. You'll notice it.

**[SHOT: Close-up of Razer keycap stem.]**

**SUBTITLE:** Proprietary stems. Standard keycaps won't fit.

**VO:** You also can't swap keycaps without adapters. Razer uses their own stem design. At £180 to £220, you're locking yourself in. If you're already deep in the Razer ecosystem with a mouse and headset — fine, it makes sense. If you're not buying anything else from them, think twice.

---

### KEYCHRON K2 HE

**SUBTITLE:** KEYCHRON K2 HE — THE VALUE KING (WITH CAVEATS)

**[SHOT: Keychron K2 HE. Wireless mode demo — unplug cable, type wirelessly.]**

**SUBTITLE (bullet points):**
- Hall effect + rapid trigger at £100–130
- Only wireless hall effect board here
- Hot-swap sockets — change switches anytime
- But: plastic case, cramped 75% layout, Bluetooth latency gaming

**VO:** This is the smartest purchase in the roundup. Same hall effect tech as the Wooting. Same rapid trigger. Add wireless — the only wireless hall effect board here — and hot-swap sockets that let you drop in standard mechanical switches whenever you want. The Gateron Nebula switches sound deeper than the Wooting's Lekkers too.

**[SHOT: Hands typing on the 75% layout. Show cramped Fn combos.]**

**SUBTITLE:** 75% layout. Home and End require Fn key combos.

**VO:** The plastic case feels exactly like what it costs. The 75% layout will annoy you if you're used to TKL or full-size — home and end require Fn combinations. And Bluetooth latency is noticeable in fast-paced games. You'll use it wired for gaming, which defeats half the point. But at £100 to £130, you're getting 85% of the Wooting experience for 60% of the price. That's a good deal.

---

### CORSAIR K70 CORE

**SUBTITLE:** CORSAIR K70 CORE — THE SENSIBLE CHOICE

**[SHOT: Corsair K70 CORE. Media controls demo — volume roller, mute button.]**

**SUBTITLE (bullet points):**
- Proper mechanical switches under £90
- Media controls work without software
- Aluminium top plate — doesn't flex
- But: no rapid trigger, soldered switches, cheap keycaps

**VO:** If you don't play competitive FPS at a level where milliseconds matter, this is probably the keyboard you should buy. Genuine mechanical switches. Dedicated media controls that just work — no software required. Solid aluminium plate. It's a keyboard that does keyboard things and gets out of your way.

**[SHOT: Close-up of keycaps. Show laser-etched legends.]**

**SUBTITLE:** ABS keycaps. Legends will fade.

**VO:** The keycaps are poor — ABS with laser-etched legends that will wear. The switches are soldered, so if one dies after warranty, you're replacing the whole board. And there's no rapid trigger. For £70 to £90, you're getting a well-built mechanical keyboard with zero competitive gaming features. Know what you're trading.

---

### STEELSERIES APEX PRO TKL

**SUBTITLE:** STEELSERIES APEX PRO TKL — THE GIMMICK TAX

**[SHOT: SteelSeries Apex Pro TKL. OLED display demo — game stats, Discord notification, GIF.]**

**SUBTITLE (bullet points):**
- Hall effect with aluminium frame
- OLED display — useful for 1 week
- Dual actuation — walk on light press, sprint on full
- But: gritty switch feel, requires SteelSeries GG, USB passthrough is USB 2.0

**VO:** Premium aluminium build. Hall effect switches. And a little OLED screen that you will use for exactly one week and then forget exists. The dual actuation feature is genuinely clever — walk on a light press, sprint on a full press — but most games don't support it natively. You're configuring per-game profiles for a feature you'll use in maybe two titles.

**[SHOT: Close-up of OmniPoint switch. Slow press showing actuation point on screen.]**

**SUBTITLE:** OmniPoint 2.0. Slightly gritty on slow presses vs Wooting Lekkers.

**VO:** The OmniPoint switches feel slightly gritty on slow presses compared to the Wooting's Lekkers. Small thing. But at £170 to £190, small things matter. The OLED adds cost for a feature most people ignore. And the USB passthrough on the keyboard is USB 2.0 — can't even power a modern mouse properly. If you want the SteelSeries ecosystem for your headset too, it makes sense. Otherwise, the Wooting does the same job cleaner.

**SUBTITLE:** THE WINNER: WOOTING 80HE

**SUBTITLE:** 9.3 / 10

**VO:** The Wooting 80HE is the best gaming keyboard of 2026. Not because it has the most features — it doesn't. Not because it's the best built — the SteelSeries is arguably more premium. It wins because it delivers the cleanest competitive gaming experience. Hall effect switches with the best rapid trigger implementation. 8,000Hz polling. And Wootility runs in a browser — no install, no bloat, no background process eating your CPU. At £180 to £200, the compromises are real — no wireless, mediocre keycaps, and rapid trigger that's actively bad for typing. But for pure gaming performance, nothing currently beats it.

**SUBTITLE:** BEST VALUE: KEYCHRON K2 HE | 8.7 / 10

**SUBTITLE:** 85% of the Wooting. 60% of the price. Plus wireless.

**VO:** If £180 is too much — and for most people, it is — buy the Keychron K2 HE. Same hall effect technology. Same rapid trigger. Plus wireless and hot-swap sockets. You give up the aluminium chassis and the polished software, but at £100 to £130, you're getting the features that actually matter.
